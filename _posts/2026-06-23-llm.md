ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude — you've heard the names. They're all **Large Language Models**, or LLMs. But what does that actually mean? And how does a piece of software produce text that sounds like a human wrote it?

Let's break it down without the jargon.

## It Starts With Prediction

At its core, an LLM does one thing: it predicts the next word.

Given the sentence *"The capital of France is..."*, it predicts *"Paris"*. Given *"She opened the door and..."*, it predicts something that fits — *"walked in"*, *"screamed"*, whatever makes sense in context.

That sounds simple. But do this billions of times, across hundreds of billions of words of text, and something remarkable happens: the model starts to develop a deep sense of language, facts, reasoning patterns, even tone.

## How It Learns

LLMs are trained on massive amounts of text — books, websites, code, articles. During training, the model reads a sentence, tries to predict the next word, checks if it got it right, and adjusts its internal settings slightly based on the error. Repeat this trillions of times.

The model never memorises text word for word. Instead, it learns *patterns* — grammar, facts, how arguments are structured, how questions are typically answered.

## What "Large" Actually Means

The "large" in Large Language Model refers to the number of **parameters** — the internal numerical settings the model adjusts during training. GPT-4 is estimated to have over a trillion parameters. More parameters generally means more capacity to capture nuance and handle complex tasks.

More parameters also means more computing power needed, which is why training frontier models costs tens of millions of dollars.

## Why They Sound So Human

Because they're trained on human-written text. The model has seen how humans explain things, argue, joke, apologise, and teach. It learned to mirror those patterns.

It doesn't *understand* in the way you do. There's no meaning behind the words for the model — just statistical relationships between tokens. But those relationships are rich enough to produce output that's often indistinguishable from human writing.

## The Limitation to Keep in Mind

LLMs are frozen at training time. They don't learn from your conversations, and they don't know what happened after their training cutoff. That's exactly why RAG (see the previous post) exists — to give them access to fresh, specific information at query time.
